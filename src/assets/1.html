Phase of development: 3

Objectives: To evaluate the efficacy, pharmacokinetics, and safety of icatibant for the treatment of acute attacks in Japanese subjects with type I or type II hereditary angioedema (HAE).

Methodology:

This Phase 3 study was an uncontrolled, open-label, single-arm study to evaluate the efficacy and safety of icatibant as a treatment for acute attacks of HAE in Japanese subjects. In addition, the study evaluated the pharmacokinetic (PK) properties of icatibant in Japanese subjects with HAE. It was conducted as a multicenter study with sites in Japan. The study enrolled male and female Japanese subjects with HAE who were 18 years of age and older.

Subjects may have been screened at any time before enrollment. Following informed consent and completion of screening procedures, subjects entered into a waiting period until the onset of an HAE attack. An adequate number of subjects were screened to provide eligible subjects with a confirmed diagnosis of HAE who presented with an acute non-laryngeal or laryngeal attack within 6 hours of the symptoms of the attack becoming moderate to severe (abdominal and/or cutaneous attacks) or mild to moderate (laryngeal attacks), but not more than 12 hours from the onset of the attack.

Enrollment and treatment occurred only when subjects presented with cutaneous and/or abdominal symptoms which were judged to be at least moderate or with laryngeal symptoms that were judged to be at least mild within the protocol-specified time frame. The location and severity of the attack at baseline was determined by the investigator global assessment.

Enrolled subjects received treatment with icatibant for 1 acute non-laryngeal or laryngeal attack. It was planned that treatment would consist of 1 subcutaneous (SC) injection of icatibant at a dose of 30 mg; however, a maximum of 3 SC injections of icatibant that were at least 6 hours apart could have been given for treatment of an attack if, within 48 hours of the initial treatment, there was insufficient relief or worsening of symptoms. If symptoms worsened or recurred more than 48 hours after the initial treatment, the event was considered as a new attack. A new attack was not treated with icatibant, but rather, was managed according to the current HAE standard of care in Japan.

Doses were administered by a healthcare professional (HCP) or self-administered by the subject at the hospital/clinic

under the supervision of an HCP. Subjects were encouraged to self-administer icatibant under supervision and received training on the appropriate self-injection technique from an HCP prior to treatment. This training was

The sparse PK samples were taken from each subject at 0.75 and 2 hours post SC administration. The plasma concentrations of icatibant and its 2 metabolites M1 and M2 were determined by validated bioanalysis method.

The icatibant plasma concentrations from this study were included in the population PK analysis along with those from previous studies in healthy subjects and non-Japanese subjects with HAE. The effect of covariates (ie, age, body weight, sex, race, disease state) on the PK of icatibant was evaluated and a population PK model was established. Icatibant PK parameters (ie, maximum observed plasma concentration [Cmax], area under the curve [AUC]) were estimated by a population PK modeling approach.

Safety Endpoints

The safety and tolerability of icatibant was evaluated through the assessment of AEs, including clinically relevant worsening of the signs and symptoms of a treated HAE attack, injection site reactions, clinical laboratory testing (hematology, serum chemistry, coagulation, and urinalysis), vital sign measurements, electrocardiogram (ECG), and physical examination.

Statistical methods:

The primary efficacy endpoint, TOSR, was the time from investigational product administration to onset of symptom relief, where onset of symptom relief was defined as the earliest of 3 consecutive non-missing measurements for which there is at least a 50% reduction in the pretreatment composite VAS score. The composite VAS score was defined as the average of individual VAS scores for 3 symptoms (skin swelling, skin pain, and abdominal pain) for non-laryngeal attacks and 5 symptoms (skin swelling, skin pain, abdominal pain, difficulty swallowing, and voice change) for laryngeal attacks. Time to onset of symptom relief was summarized using the Kaplan-Meier method.

The key secondary efficacy endpoint was time to onset of primary symptom relief (TOSR-P), which was calculated from the time of icatibant administration to the onset of primary symptom relief. Onset of primary symptom relief was determined retrospectively as the earliest of 3 consecutive non-missing measurements in which there was a reduction less than Y=6/7 X  16 if X ≥30 or 68% if X <30 mm in the pretreatment primary VAS score. Time to onset of primary symptom relief was summarized using the Kaplan-Meier method.

Other secondary endpoints (time to almost complete symptom relief [TACSR], time to initial symptom improvement [TISI], and time to onset of symptom relief for individual VAS symptoms) were also summarized using the Kaplan-Meier method. Other secondary efficacy endpoints, including change from baseline when appropriate, were summarized descriptively.

Plasma samples were assayed for concentrations of icatibant and its 2 major metabolites (M1 and M2). Results:

Demographics and Baseline Characteristics

A total of 8 subjects were treated with icatibant: 3 subjects self-administered icatibant and 5 subjects had icatibant administered by an HCP.

The mean (SD) age was 45.5 (16.9) years (range 28.3 to 78.0). Seven (87.5%) of 8 subjects were in the ≤65-year-old age group. All subjects were Asian, and 5 were female (62.5%). The mean (SD) weight was 72.0 (20.3) kg (range 47.3 to 108.0), and 4 (50%) subjects were in the >50 to 75 kg weight category. The mean (SD) height was 162.0 (7.8) cm (range 150.0 to 176.0), and the mean (SD) body mass index (BMI) was 27.4 (7.3) kg/m2 (range 19.3 to 36.3).

Hereditary angioedema history was summarized for the treated population. Four (50.0%) of 8 subjects reported a family history of HAE. The mean (SD) time since diagnosis was 10.16 (9.21) years, and the mean (SD) time since the last attack was 3.98 (2.61) months.

The on-study HAE attack was summarized for the treated population. A total of 4 (50.0%) subjects were experiencing a cutaneous attack, 3 (37.5%) subjects were experiencing an abdominal attack, and 1 (12.5%) subject experienced a laryngeal attack. All 8 (100.0%) treated subjects completed the study. The mean time (SD) from attack onset to study drug administration was 6.50 (2.37) hours (range 3.1 to 10.0). The administration method groups were similar with respect to on-study HAE attacks.

A total of 6 (75.0%) of 8 subjects used a concomitant medication during their participation in the trial.

Rescue Medication: One subject used a medication designated by the investigator as rescue medication. However, this medication does not meet the protocol definition of rescue medication as it was given for a new abdominal HAE attack of moderate severity that occurred more than 48 hours after the initial attack.

At 1.5 hours post-treatment, 4 (50%) of 8 subjects had experienced symptom relief and at 2.5 hours post-treatment, 7 (87.5%) of 8 subjects had experienced symptom relief.

Safety Results

There were a total of 3 TEAEs in 3 (37.5%) of 8 subjects. Two (25%) subjects experienced TEAEs of HAE and 1 (12.5%) subject experienced a TEAE of a headache. The 3 TEAEs reported in this study were assessed as mild in intensity and not related to the study drug by the investigator.

Seven (87.5%) of 8 subjects experienced an injection site reaction. The most frequently noted injection site reactions were erythema in 7 (87.5%) subjects, swelling in 7 (87.5%) subjects, and warm sensation in 4 (50.0%) subjects.

Almost all injection site reactions had resolved by 8 hours postdose. Injection site reactions were mild or moderate in intensity. One (12.5%) subject experienced a severe injection site reaction (erythema) which resolved by 2 hours post-treatment.

There were no deaths, no serious adverse reactions (SAEs), and no discontinuations due to a TEAE in this study. None of the TEAEs suggested a deterioration of cardiac function. No clinically meaningful changes were observed in clinical laboratory values, vital signs, or ECGs.

Other Observations Related to Safety

Self-administration of Study Drug: The administration method groups were similar with respect to the summary of all TEAEs. With regards to injection site reactions, the administration methods were generally similar (2 of 3 subjects who self-administered icatibant; 5 of 5 subjects who had icatibant administered by an HCP). Erythema was the only severe injection site reaction that was present in 1 subject who had icatibant administered by an HCP. The administration method groups were generally similar with respect to all other safety endpoints.

Conclusions

Icatibant is an efficacious treatment for acute attacks of HAE including cutaneous, abdominal, and laryngeal attacks, as demonstrated by the reproducibility and consistency of efficacy across multiple endpoints, in the open-label study of icatibant in adult Japanese subjects. Icatibant provided rapid resolution of symptoms. Clinically meaningful efficacy in the primary and all secondary endpoints was demonstrated.

Systemic exposure to icatibant in Japanese subjects was demonstrated and was found to induce sufficient clinical response.

Icatibant was generally well tolerated, and no new safety signals were identified in adult Japanese subjects with HAE. The safety profile of icatibant in adult Japanese subjects with HAE was favorable and consistent with the disease and with icatibant safety profile in adult non-Japanese subjects with HAE.

The efficacy and safety endpoints were generally similar in subjects who self-administered icatibant compared with subjects who had icatibant administered by an HCP.

The treatment response to icatibant in adult Japanese subjects experiencing an acute attack of HAE was consistent with the treatment response in adult non-Japanese subjects.

In conclusion, this study demonstrated that icatibant can address the unmet medical need for a safe and effective treatment for acute attacks of HAE in adult Japanese subjects.
